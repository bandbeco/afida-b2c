Branded disposable packaging turns every order into a marketing touchpoint. A cup, bag, or box carrying your logo leaves your premises in a customer's hand and may travel through a high street, a park, or an office. Done well, branded packaging builds recognition and reinforces quality. Done poorly, it signals a mismatch between the brand you present online and what customers actually receive. For food service businesses considering the investment, the key is understanding what branded packaging can realistically deliver and where plain stock with a simple label is a better use of budget.

## Key Factors to Consider

### Which Products to Brand

Not everything needs your logo on it. Prioritise items with the most customer visibility and the longest journey outside your premises. Hot cups, cold cups, carrier bags, and boxes are the highest-impact choices. Napkins, cutlery wraps, and greaseproof sheets are secondary but useful if your operation has a strong aesthetic. Deli pots and food containers are usually not worth the minimum order quantity unless you are supplying retail or subscription products.

### Print Methods and Quality

The main print options for disposable food packaging are:

- **Flexographic printing:** The standard for paper cups, bags, and boxes at volume. Cost-effective for one to three colours. Minimum orders typically start at 5,000 to 10,000 units for cups, lower for bags.
- **Digital printing:** Better for shorter runs (500 to 2,000 units), full-colour designs, and seasonal or limited-edition lines. Unit cost is higher but minimum quantities are lower.
- **Labels and stickers applied to plain stock:** The lowest-commitment option. Allows stock flexibility (you can use the same cup across multiple product lines) and much lower minimum quantities. Useful for new businesses or low-volume operators.

Print quality matters. Ask your supplier for physical samples of printed products, not just digital renders. Colours shift significantly between screen and print, especially on kraft brown substrates.

### Minimum Order Quantities

MOQs for custom printed packaging vary by product and print method:

- Printed paper cups: typically 1,000 to 5,000 units minimum
- Printed carrier bags: often 500 to 2,000 units minimum
- Printed greaseproof paper: typically 5,000 sheets minimum
- Printed boxes: 500 to 2,000 units depending on format

If you cannot move through a minimum order quantity within three to six months, the storage and cash-flow cost offsets the branding benefit. Be realistic about your volumes.

### Artwork and Lead Times

Printed packaging requires print-ready artwork. If you are working from a logo file, you need a vector format (EPS or PDF with outlined fonts, minimum 300dpi for any raster elements). Lead times for first-time printed orders typically run four to eight weeks, including proofing. Repeat orders are faster once artwork is approved. Plan ahead for seasonal campaigns or new product launches -- last-minute branded packaging is expensive when rushed.

### Storage

Branded stock commits you to a design. If your branding, name, or contact details change, you are left with unusable packaging. Order in quantities you will consume within six to twelve months. Consider climate-controlled storage for paper products, as humidity can damage printed packaging.

### Cost and Value

Branded cups at volume (5,000+) might add £0.03 to £0.10 per unit compared to plain equivalents. At 500 coffees a week, that is £15 to £50 per week in additional cost. Whether that buys enough brand exposure to justify the spend depends on your context -- a high street cafe with visible takeaway traffic will see more return than a business that delivers primarily to offices.

## Pro Tips

- Start with a branded carrier bag before committing to cups or boxes. The bag travels the furthest and has the largest surface area for your logo. It is also one of the lower-commitment branded items in terms of MOQ.
- Always request a physical proof before approving a print run. Digital colour approvals look different on screen than on actual kraft paper or PE-coated board.
- If you are early in your branding journey, labels on quality plain stock are often the right move. A well-designed label on a clean white cup looks better than a poorly printed branded cup.
- Brief your designer on the substrate colour before they start artwork. Dark logos get lost on kraft brown; white or light colours often need a white ink underprint, which adds cost.

## Summary

Branded packaging is a genuine investment in visibility and brand consistency, but it requires realistic volume planning, quality artwork, and careful product selection. Start with high-visibility items, choose the print method that matches your volumes, and never approve a print run without seeing a physical sample. For low-volume operations, high-quality labels on plain stock is a sound and flexible alternative.
